Sketch, engagement landing

Layout explorations for the engagement home page.

The current home is mostly standardized after the recent typography unification, but "Latest from Stout" is still a naked section heading sitting on the page background while the other four widgets are bordered cards with an inset header divider. Six options below: a baseline frame plus five directional alternatives.

Each frame uses simplified rows. The point is to compare header treatment and structural shape, not to ship pixel-perfect content. Annotations call out what's changing and what the cost is.

A

Baseline, today

Current state. Four widgets share the unified header (10.5px / 700 / slate-700 / 0.18em uppercase / inset slate-100 divider, inside a bordered card). "Latest from Stout" sits on the page background with a heavier slate-200 underline, and the items below it have their own separately bordered list. Two boundary treatments compete in the same column.

Why this works One container shape, one divider weight, one action affordance. "Latest from Stout" keeps its emphasis through size and position, not through breaking the grammar.
Trade-off More containment means a slightly heavier feel. The widget that was visually most "open" becomes another bordered box. Accept this cost or compensate with a touch more whitespace (gap up from 18px to 22px between cards).
C

Editorial demote, lose the card borders

Inverse of B. Strip card borders entirely from the main column. Each section is a header row with the slate-100 underline pattern, and the rows below sit directly on the page background, separated by hairlines. Side rail keeps light containment because it's a reference panel, not a feed.

E

Equal two-column, drop the side rail

Replace the 1fr / 320px asymmetric grid with a 50/50 split. Left column is "what happened" (What changed, Latest from Stout). Right column is "what's coming, what's open" (Drafts, Next 14 days, Recent materials). On a wide monitor the side rail today feels narrow; this makes both columns full-fidelity reading surfaces.

Why this works Pairs intent: left = backward-looking (what happened), right = forward-looking (what's open, what's coming). Symmetry helps scanning at desktop widths.
Trade-off Loses the implicit hierarchy that "the wide column is the primary content." Both columns get equal visual weight, which makes the page feel more balanced but less directed. On narrower screens the right column drops to ~360px, which is fine, but on wide it has more horizontal real estate than its compact rows need.
F

Stat strip becomes filters

A different lever. Today the context strip stats are display-only. Make them clickable chips that scope the feed below: click "Catalysts < 90d" and the column filters to markers in that window; click "Companies" and the column groups by company. Turns the header into a control surface.

Brand risk This converts a calm display surface into a control. The home page starts to feel like a query builder rather than a briefing. Users may also expect the rest of the page to react (drafts, materials), which expands the surface area significantly.
Trade-off Probably the wrong move for this product's tone. The instrument should answer first and let the user dig in deliberately, not invite quick filtering at the top. Worth flagging but not recommending unless we redefine what the home is for.
Summary, recommendation

B (unify all five) is the lowest-risk fix for the headers question on its own. It's a 6-line CSS change to the "Latest from Stout" section: wrap it in the same bordered card and swap the divider to slate-100.

D (timeline hero) is the most interesting if we want to reconsider what the page is for. The current home is a feed dashboard; D treats it as an engagement instrument with the timeline up top. It changes the answer to "what is this page" not just "how do its cards look."

C and E are tone shifts; pick C for editorial calm, E for symmetric scanning. F is here for completeness but probably wrong for the brand.
