02 / What Clint is

A pharma-native intelligence platform — structured, event-centric, AI-ready.

  • Pharma-native data model. Pipelines, assets, clinical trials, catalysts, and portfolios are first-class objects — not free-text notes or PDF dumps.
  • Event-centric. Every meaningful change — data readouts, regulatory actions, approvals, label changes, deal activity — attaches to the right asset as a typed, timestamped, source-cited marker.
  • Built for instant parsing. Visual phase bars, color-coded markers, and a company-and-product hierarchy that lets a reader land on the competitive read in seconds, not minutes.
  • Structured underneath, not just on the surface. The interface is one consumer of the data; exports, briefing decks, client notices, and AI workflows are others. Same source of truth.
  • AI-ready data shape. Events are normalized and citation-backed at ingest — the cleanup tax of any AI workflow, already paid. The structured layer is what API access, MCP integration, and Clint-native AI all build on.

04 / Origin & IP

Built independently. IP is clean.

Insider channel without insider IP risk. The structure has to be airtight before a single MD conversation.

Origin
  • Partner at Stout (CI practice, ownership stake) flagged the recurring pain points and suggested someone build a tool around them.
  • Founder took the suggestion and built Clint independently — code, platform, brand, and IP.
  • Partner's contribution: ideation, domain validation, ongoing advisory. Not co-development.
IP ownership
  • Code, copyright, trademarks, data model = founder.
  • Ideas are not protectable IP under US law — implementation is. Nothing partner contributed creates a Stout claim.
  • Mitigation: founder/advisor agreement before any Stout-side conversation, formalizing partner's stake and confirming no Stout time, systems, client data, or deliverables were used.
  • Cheap insurance. Also makes the COI disclosure (next) credible.

05 / Conflict of interest

Disclose, don't conceal. Disclosure strengthens the pitch.

  • Partner discloses Clint economic interest to the MD on the record — in the first three minutes of the first conversation. Not buried, not hinted at.
  • Partner does not negotiate Stout-side pricing or terms. Partner makes the introduction; founder (or a third party) negotiates.
  • Partner recuses from internal Stout decision meetings where the Clint deal is being evaluated for purchase.

06 / Partner → MD conversation flow

Exploratory, insider-to-insider, honest about state.

  1. Open with the gap, not the product. "We've talked before about how much time the CI team burns on assembly before we get to the strategic read. Same pattern across engagements."
  2. Frame the origin honestly — this is the COI disclosure. "I floated the idea a while back. [Founder] is the one who actually built it. He owns the IP. I'll have a small stake — want to be upfront before I show you anything."
  3. What it is, in one sentence. "A pharma CI platform with structured pipelines, catalysts, and trial data — analyst time on the strategic read, whitelabel for client deliverables."
  4. What's built today. Live multi-tenant platform. Whitelabel agency/tenant/engagement branding plus custom domains. Structured event model. AI-ready data layer. Be honest about gaps: data coverage, therapeutic depth, SOC 2.
  5. Where it fits at Stout. Internal: assembly compression. Client-facing: branded live environment, not a static deck. Strategic: ahead of the advisory market on AI-ready CI.
  6. The ask. 30-minute walkthrough with founder. If it lands, scope a paid pilot on one live engagement.
  7. Soft "why now." "He's going to keep building this. Wanted Stout to have first look."

07 / What NOT to say in the first conversation

Four landmines.

  • No price. The MD will instinctively benchmark to Evaluate ($20–50K/seat, $100K+/yr enterprise). Pricing comes after they've seen the product.
  • No exclusivity offer. That is a structured discussion after pilot, with the right people in the room.
  • No downplaying the COI. Disclose, on the record, in the first three minutes. If it surfaces later, the deal is contaminated.
  • No "we are talking to your competitors." Even if true. The whole point of the partner channel is that this is offered to Stout first.

10 / Exclusivity without losing IP

Stout gets a moat. Clint keeps the asset.

  • License the right to use, never the IP itself. Standard SaaS framing — Clint retains all IP; Stout receives a non-transferable, non-sublicensable right to use within agreed scope.
  • Whitelabel ≠ IP transfer. Stout's branding sits on the surface clients see. Platform, code, data model, and brand below the whitelabel stay with Clint.
  • Exclusivity is a contract term, not an IP transfer. Time-bound, category-bound, fully reversible: "Clint will not license to other firms in [category] during [2–3 yr] term."
  • Reverse termination right. If Stout fails to renew or hit a usage minimum, exclusivity lapses immediately. Protects against the "buy and park" failure mode.
  • Carve-outs from day one. Pharma/biotech direct sales explicitly permitted. Define the category narrowly — "global advisory firms whose primary offering includes pharma transaction support, valuation, or CI consulting."
  • No source code, no data ownership transfer. Even under exclusivity. Stout gets access to use the platform, not access to fork it.

13 / Pain points · the wedge

Buyers already articulate the gaps Clint closes.

What buyers complain about
  • Clunky interfaces. Universal complaint across the big three.
  • Stale and inaccurate data. GlobalData specifically. Generalized: "miss at least 20% of updated information" if you trust just one platform.
  • Forced multi-platform redundancy. Buyers run two or more and manually cross-check.
  • Aggressive sales tactics. GlobalData — "the most aggressive, obnoxious salesforce in our biz."
  • Opaque pricing. No public benchmark, every deal is bespoke.
  • Vendor instability. Cortellis ownership has changed multiple times.
What this tells us
  • Coverage gap is the wedge. Clint does not need to beat Evaluate at depth on day one — it needs to be the layer where Stout's curated, annotated view sits on top.
  • UX is a real differentiator. Buyers themselves call these tools clunky.
  • Provenance is price-of-entry. Source citations on every event are required for advisory firm output.
  • Sell against opacity. Transparent pricing tiers are themselves a differentiator.
  • Whitelabel + AI-ready is the combination none of them offer.

14 / Anticipated objection · delivery model

"Won't clients see how the pudding is made?"

The honest answer: a live platform commoditizes the assembly work. It does not commoditize the strategic read — unless that was all Stout was ever delivering.

Make Stout's IP visible inside the platform
  • Curation as a deliverable. Watchlist, competitor set, asset prioritization — Stout's domain expertise applied.
  • Annotation as a deliverable. Every meaningful event carries a Stout note — why it matters, what it shifts.
  • Strategy surface as a deliverable. Scenario analysis, deal implications, recommendations — branded as Stout's work.
  • Decks shrink, not disappear. Quarterly briefings become "here is what we recommend you do," not "here is what happened." More senior, higher-margin artifact.
Defuse it with structure
  • Pricing posture. Never expose the platform as a SaaS line item. Bundle inside the CI retainer. "Competitive Intelligence Program," not "Platform license + advisory hours."
  • Sales framing. Position the platform as Stout's instrument, not the product.
  • Switching cost rises. Watchlists, annotations, history in the Stout-branded environment mean leaving = losing institutional memory. Static decks have zero switching cost.
  • Visibility helps. Continuous activity is perceived as more value than one polished deck per quarter.
