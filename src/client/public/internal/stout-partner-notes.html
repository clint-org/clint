Partner brief: prep for the Stout MD conversation.

Talking points, what to disclose, what not to say, and how to handle pricing or exclusivity if they come up. This is the prep doc for the partner. The MD sees the briefing deck; the MD does not see this.

Clint Partner notes
01 / Origin and IP

What was built, by whom, with what.

This needs to be airtight before the MD conversation, because the COI disclosure is only credible if the IP picture is clean.

Origin
  • The partner (CI practice, ownership stake in Stout) flagged recurring pain points and suggested someone build a tool around them.
  • The founder built Clint independently. Code, platform, brand, and IP belong to the founder.
  • The partner contributes ideas, validation, and ongoing advisory. Not co-development.
IP ownership
  • Code, copyright, trademarks, and data model belong to the founder.
  • Ideas aren't protectable IP under US law — implementation is. Nothing the partner contributed creates a Stout claim on the asset.
  • Required before any Stout-side conversation: founder/advisor agreement. Formalizes the partner's stake. Confirms in writing that no Stout time, systems, client data, or deliverables were used to build the platform.
  • This is cheap insurance. It's also what makes the COI disclosure on the next slide credible.

02 / Conflict of interest

Disclose, don't conceal.

The partner has Stout equity (existing) and will have Clint upside (new). The COI is real, and the way to handle it is full disclosure on the record.

  • Disclose the Clint stake to the MD on the record — in the first three minutes of the conversation. Not buried, not hinted at.
  • Don't negotiate Stout-side pricing or terms. Make the introduction. The founder (or a third party) handles negotiation.
  • Recuse from internal Stout meetings where the Clint deal is being evaluated for purchase.
"I have a stake in this and I'm bringing it to you anyway because I think it's right for the firm" is a stronger opening than a salesperson cold-calling. The MD is being told this is an insider's recommendation, made transparently. That's a feature, not a problem to manage. The framing

03 / The MD conversation, in seven steps

Exploratory, peer-to-peer, honest about state.

  1. Open with the gap, not the product. "We've talked before about how much time the CI team burns on assembly before we get to the strategic read. Same pattern across engagements."
  2. Frame the origin honestly. This is the COI disclosure. "I floated the idea a while back. [Founder] is the one who actually built it. He owns the IP. I'll have a small stake in it — want to be upfront before I show you anything."
  3. What it is, in one sentence. "A pharma CI platform with structured pipelines, catalysts, and trial data — analyst time on the strategic read, whitelabel for client deliverables."
  4. What's built today. Live multi-tenant platform. Whitelabel agency / tenant / engagement branding plus custom domains. Structured event model. AI-ready data shape. Be honest about gaps: data coverage, therapeutic depth, SOC 2 status.
  5. Where it fits at Stout. Internal: assembly compression. Client-facing: branded live environment, not a static deck. Strategic: ahead of the advisory market on AI-ready CI.
  6. The ask. 30-minute walkthrough with the founder. If it lands, scope a paid pilot on one live engagement.
  7. Soft "why now." "He's going to keep building this. Wanted Stout to have first look." Don't threaten. Just signal the optionality is real.

04 / What NOT to say in the first conversation

Four things to keep off the table.

  • No price. If asked, redirect: "Pricing is shaped from how Stout would actually use it. Let's get through the walkthrough first." The MD will benchmark to Evaluate ($20–50K/seat, $100K+/yr enterprise) the moment a number lands. Don't hand them that comparison.
  • No exclusivity offer. That's a structured discussion after the pilot, with the right people in the room. Don't promise it in the hallway.
  • No downplaying the COI. Disclose, on the record, in the first three minutes. If it surfaces later, the deal is contaminated.
  • No "we're talking to your competitors." Even if it's true. The whole point of the partner channel is that this is offered to Stout first.

Pilot, then license with an exclusivity option. Defer acquisition.

07 / Exclusivity without giving up IP

Stout gets a moat. The founder keeps the asset.

  • License the right to use, not the IP itself. Standard SaaS framing. Clint retains all IP. Stout gets a non-transferable, non-sublicensable right to use within agreed scope.
  • Whitelabel isn't IP transfer. Stout's branding sits on the surface clients see. The platform, code, data model, and brand below the whitelabel stay with Clint.
  • Exclusivity is a contract term, not an IP transfer. Time-bound, category-bound, fully reversible. "Clint will not license to other firms in [defined advisory category] during the [2–3 year] term."
  • Reverse termination right. If Stout fails to renew or hit a usage minimum, exclusivity lapses immediately. Protects against Stout buying exclusivity, parking the asset, and killing its market.
  • Define the category narrowly. "Global advisory firms whose primary offering includes pharma transaction support, valuation, or CI consulting." Pharma and biotech direct sales are explicitly carved out.
  • No source code, no data ownership transfer. Even under exclusivity. Stout gets to use the platform, not to fork it.

09 / Before the meeting

Eight things to lock down first.

  1. Sign the founder/advisor agreement between the founder and the partner. Formalizes IP ownership, the partner's stake, and the disclosure framework. Has to happen first.
  2. Practice the disclosure script from slide 4 step 2. It needs to land in the first three minutes and feel natural, not rehearsed.
  3. Decide pilot scope and price — fixed-fee, 60–90 days, $25K–$50K. So if the MD asks "what would a trial look like," there's an answer ready.
  4. Identify the right MD. Name, practice focus, recent pharma CI / valuation / M&A engagements they've worked on.
  5. Pick the demo landscape. A therapeutic area or competitor set Stout has live work in — so the walkthrough lands on something the MD already cares about.
  6. Prepare the data coverage answer. Which sources are in, which are gaps, what the gap-fill plan is. The MD will ask.
  7. Prepare the SOC 2 / provenance answer. What's true today vs. what's on the roadmap. Advisory firms always ask.
  8. Confirm the MD-facing briefing URL works on the partner's laptop and phone before the meeting. /internal/stout-briefing.html
