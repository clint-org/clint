## Part 0 — The 90 seconds that matter

### The one-sentence version
> "Clint is a whitelabel platform that gives your client live competitive intelligence the moment it's created — under Stout's brand — with a growing toolset on top."

### The mental model (say this and the whole product clicks)
> "It doesn't replace the data tools Stout already runs. It's the **layer where Stout's read sits on top of them** — where the CI work lives, where deliverables get filed, and where the client sees it all in real time."

That one line defuses the reflex objection ("we already have Evaluate / Citeline"). Clint is not another data vendor. It's the surface Stout delivers *through*.

### The data hierarchy (memorize — everything hangs off this)
**Company → Asset → Trial → Marker**, with **Catalyst** = a marker in the future.
- **Company** — a pharma firm (Lilly, Novo, Pfizer, Roche, Cytokinetics, Viking).
- **Asset** — one drug a company owns (orforglipron, retatrutide, CagriSema, aficamten).
- **Trial** — a clinical study testing an asset (ATTAIN-1, REDEFINE-2). Has an **NCT number** (its ClinicalTrials.gov registry ID).
- **Marker** — a dated event on a trial's timeline (a readout, a milestone, a regulatory action). The colored dots. The foreground of the whole product.
- **Catalyst** — a *future* marker that can move the market (a topline readout, an FDA decision date).

### The ice-breaker (use the Bloomberg one)
> "One way to frame what you're about to see. A trader doesn't get a PDF of where the markets sat last Tuesday — they get a live terminal, current to the second, and that's why nobody on a trading floor can work without one. Competitive intelligence in pharma is still delivered like the Tuesday PDF: a deck that's accurate the day you hand it over and quietly out of date a week later. What I want to show you is the terminal version of the work Stout already does — same read, but live, in the client's hands every day, with Stout's name on it."

Alternates if the room wants something lighter: a **live map vs. a printed MapQuest printout** (useless the moment a road closes), or — only if the MD is informal — *"a CI deck is sushi: premium the day it's made, and you don't want it three weeks later."*

### Three rules for yourself
1. **No dollar figure on a slide or unprompted.** If pushed: *"$25K per engagement space, capped at $300K across the whole firm — less than a single Evaluate seat, and it bundles into your fee, not a line item the client sees."* Then stop.
2. **Don't volunteer the partner's stake.** If asked directly: yes, a small advisory stake; the intro is happening because it's the right tool, not because of the stake. Brief, honest, move on.
3. **The ask is small:** hands on the platform this week, one live engagement, shaped around how Stout works. Not a contract, not exclusivity — that's a later conversation, and only if *they* open it.

## Part 2 — Feature deep-dives

For each surface: **What it is · Why it matters · Know this · Say this (demo script) · You'll be asked · Steer around.** The five Landscape views (Home / Timeline / Bullseye / Heatmap / Catalysts) are **five lenses on one shared, filtered set of trial data** — filters follow you between them. Lead with that.

### 2.1 — Home (Engagement Landing)
**What it is.** The analyst's (and client's) morning screen: "what changed and what's coming" for this engagement. Read-only; everything links deeper. Route: the bare space URL.

**Why it matters.** It's the first thing the client sees every day. This is the proof of "live" — the whole pitch in one screen.

**Know this.**
- **Summary strip:** inventory totals (`35 trials · 12 companies · 27 assets`) + `Active since 2026-Q2` + an `IMPORT` entry (owner/editor only).
- **Hero catalyst:** the single most notable upcoming dated event, with the next two in a companion list. Auto-hides on quiet days (nothing within 90 days).
- **Four motion signals:** `P3 readouts next 90d` · `Catalysts next 90d` · `New reads last 7d` · `Trial moves last 30d`. The amber tint on the first two is a **conditional attention cue** — it lights amber only when the count is > 0. It is **not** the marker palette's "orange = regulatory." Don't conflate them if someone asks.
- **Latest from Stout:** the intelligence feed, filterable All / Trial / Company / Engagement.
- **Next 90 days:** the catalyst calendar; each entry tagged `PROJECTED` when the date is an estimate.
- **What changed (7d):** auto-detected trial changes (date slips, phase transitions); source `Clint`/`Detected` = system-found.
- **Recent materials:** uploaded deliverables.

**Say this.**
> "This is home base for an engagement. Top of the screen is the next catalyst — the thing most worth watching. This row is the engagement's pulse: P3 readouts and catalysts coming up, new analysis Stout published this week, trials that moved this month. Down here is the latest from Stout, the 90-day calendar, and what changed on its own from the daily sync. A client signs in and in five seconds knows what's new and what's coming — that's the difference between this and a deck."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"Is this live or a mockup?"* → Live. The daily ClinicalTrials.gov sync feeds "what changed"; the analyst's posts feed "latest from Stout."
- *"Who sees this?"* → The client, in their own brand. Stout authors; the client consumes.

**Steer around.** The headline counts in "Latest from Stout" are a known cosmetic rough edge (the count can reflect a fetch buffer, not the true total). Don't dwell on the exact number; talk about the *content*.

### 2.2 — Timeline
**What it is.** The core visualization: every trial in the engagement as a horizontal bar on one shared time axis, dated markers plotted along it, grouped Company → Asset → Trial. Read the whole field's timing at a glance.

**Why it matters.** This is the "wow" screen and the one closest to Bloomberg. It's the picture that takes an analyst hours to assemble by hand.

**Know this.**
- **Zoom:** Year / Quarter / Month / Day axis granularity.
- **Left grid columns:** ASSET · MOA · ROA · TRIAL (+ NCT number), grouped by company. **MOA** = mechanism of action (how the drug works); **ROA** = route (SC = subcutaneous injection, PO = oral pill — oral vs. injectable is a huge competitive differentiator in obesity).
- **Canvas:** the bar = a trial's phase span; shapes = markers; dashed vertical line = TODAY.
- **The competitive READ (headline strip):** an auto-generated plain-language synthesis of the *filtered* data — e.g. *"Eli Lilly leads: 4 assets, 8 at Phase 3 | 8 catalysts in next 90 days | Novo Nordisk most active (9 recent changes)."* The tool reads the field for you. This is a great beat — change a filter and watch the READ rewrite itself.
- **The legend = the master key:** **color = category**, **shape/fill = type + confidence.** Filled = confirmed/actual; hollow = projected; struck-through = "No Longer Expected." (Full color map in Part 4.)
- **Export:** top-right, to a PowerPoint briefing.

**Say this.**
> "This is the whole competitive field on one timeline — every company, every asset, every trial, grouped top to bottom. The colored shapes are dated events: green is data readouts, slate is trial milestones, orange is regulatory, blue approval, violet launch. Filled means confirmed, hollow means projected. This dashed line is today. And up here the tool has already read the field for you in plain English — [change a filter] — and it rewrites the read as I narrow it. What an analyst used to spend a morning assembling is the default view."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"Where does the data come from?"* → Trials and their dates sync daily from ClinicalTrials.gov; catalysts, regulatory events, and Stout's edits sit alongside as typed, sourced records.
- *"Can I sort the rows?"* → Today the order is the analyst's "house order" of competitors (a manual `display_order`, set under Companies). On-chart sorting (soonest catalyst first, most advanced phase) is a near-term add. *(Honest answer — don't overclaim.)*
- *"Why do some bars fade out?"* → A bar runs to the trial's completion date when we have it; if not, it feathers to today (open-ended). In a fully synced engagement they run to the real completion date.

**Steer around.** Near the today line, the "TODAY" label can overlap the year-axis label (cosmetic). Don't zoom into that corner.

### 2.3 — Bullseye
**What it is.** Competitive positioning shaped like a target. **Distance to center = closeness to market.**

**Why it matters.** Instantly answers "who's ahead?" It's the most visually striking single screen.

**Know this.**
- **Rings = development phase**, outer→in: P1 → P2 → P3 → P4 → Approved → center (Launched/on market). Rim = early; core = marketed.
- **Wedges = the grouping dimension** (default Company). Each slice is a company; its assets are dots at the ring matching their phase.
- **Dots = assets**, colored by phase (P3 = teal, the hero color). Ring decorations: hollow blue outline = "intelligence attached" (a Stout read exists); amber outline = "recent activity."
- **GROUP BY (the power control):** re-slice by Company / Indication / Mechanism of Action / Route / Asset. Switching to **MoA** answers a different question — which *mechanism* is winning (GLP-1 vs. dual vs. triple agonist), not which company.

**Say this.**
> "Same data, different question: who's closest to market. Center is launched, the rim is early-phase. Each wedge is a company, each dot one of its drugs, sitting on the ring for its phase. A blue ring means Stout has published a read on it; amber means it just moved. And this control re-slices the whole thing — by company, or [switch] by mechanism of action, which tells you which *science* is winning rather than which company. That's the analyst's strategic question, answered in one view."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"What's the difference between this and the timeline?"* → Timeline answers *when*; bullseye answers *how close to market and how crowded*. Same filtered data, different lens.

**Steer around.** The hover tooltip can sit over the chart on centrally placed dots (known positioning fix in flight). If you hover and it covers things, just move on — or click the dot to open the proper detail panel instead of relying on hover.

### 2.4 — Heatmap
**What it is.** The density / white-space view: a matrix answering "where is the competition crowded, and where are the gaps?"

**Why it matters.** This is the BD / licensing question — where's the open lane.

**Know this.**
- **Rows = competitive buckets** (the GROUP BY dimension; default MoA + Indication, e.g. "GIP/GLP-1 dual agonist + Obesity").
- **Columns = the phase pipeline** P1 / P2 / P3 / P4 / APP / LNCH / TOTAL.
- **Cells = a count, shaded by intensity** — darker = more assets there; empty = nobody. Read it as a pressure map: dark cluster = contested; empty early-phase stretch = white space; a row lit only under LNCH = a mature market.
- **COUNT toggle:** Assets / Trials / Companies (Trials = clinical activity, Assets = distinct programs).

**Say this.**
> "This is the white-space view. Rows are competitive buckets — here, mechanism plus disease — and columns are the phases. Dark means crowded, empty means open. So you can see instantly where everyone's piled in at Phase 3, and where there's a lane nobody's in yet. For a BD or licensing conversation, that empty cell is the whole point."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"Crowded by assets or by companies?"* → Toggle COUNT. Assets = distinct programs; Companies = how many players; Trials = raw clinical activity.

**Steer around.** The auto-generated READ on the heatmap is the weakest of the three (it can restate structure). Talk over the *grid* here, not the READ sentence.

### 2.5 — Future Catalysts
**What it is.** The operational watch list: the same markers as the timeline dots, presented as a chronological calendar of what fires next.

**Why it matters.** Spatial views answer "where does everyone sit"; this answers "what happens next, and when" — the thing a client checks every week.

**Know this.**
- **Layout:** time buckets of decreasing urgency — THIS WEEK → NEXT WEEK → by MONTH → by QUARTER.
- **Columns:** DATE / CATEGORY / CATALYST / COMPANY-ASSET / STATUS. STATUS = date confidence (Projected vs. Confirmed).
- **Export here is Excel** (a dated list), vs. PowerPoint on the chart views.
- Domain note: **NDA** = New Drug Application (the FDA submission). *"CagriSema NDA projected"* = Novo expected to file with the FDA.

**Say this.**
> "Everything coming up, as a calendar — this week, next week, then by month and quarter. Each row is a catalyst: a readout, a regulatory date, a milestone, color-coded by type, marked confirmed or projected. This is the page a client lives in — 'what do I need to watch this quarter.' And it exports to Excel for anyone who wants the list."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"What's 'projected'?"* → An estimated date (modeled or analyst-guided), vs. a company-confirmed one. That confirmed-vs-projected distinction runs through the whole product and is a core data-quality signal.

### 2.6 — Primary Intelligence (Stout's analysis) + History
**What it is.** Stout's actual deliverable: a structured analyst write-up with a publish lifecycle, written directly onto any trial, company, asset, or the engagement itself. **This is where Stout's IP lives in the product.**

**Why it matters.** This is the answer to "won't the client see how the sausage is made?" The platform commoditizes *assembly*; this surface is where *judgment* — the thing the client actually pays for — becomes visible and permanent.

**Know this.**
- **Card anatomy:** Title · Contributors / updated-by · **SUMMARY** (what's happening — the observation) · **IMPLICATIONS** (the "so what" / recommendation) — that split is the backbone of every post · **LINKED** (cross-references to entities, each with a note) · **HISTORY** · **MATERIALS**.
- **Lifecycle:** Draft → Published → (Archived when a newer version publishes over it) / Withdrawn. One published version per anchor; drafts can co-exist. Every publish stamps a version number.
- **History panel:** a linear timeline of every version, with **word-level diffs** between published versions — you can literally see what changed in the read from one week to the next.
- Written in a rich-text editor (bold/italic/lists); auto-saves as you type.
- **Bylines adapt:** internally it shows contributor initials; client-facing it shows just "Published by Stout, updated [date]."

**Say this.**
> "This is the actual deliverable — Stout's read, written straight onto the trial. Every post has the same backbone: what's happening, and the so-what. It links to the companies and assets it's about. And every time an analyst publishes, Clint keeps the prior version — so down here you get the full history, with the changes highlighted word by word. The client always sees the latest thinking, and anyone can look back at how the read evolved. This is the part that *isn't* commoditized: assembling the picture got automated; this is the judgment they're paying for, and now it's visible every week instead of once a quarter."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"Does the client see drafts?"* → No. Drafts are visible only to Stout (agency) members; the client only ever sees published reads.
- *"Can two analysts collaborate?"* → Yes — contributors are tracked, and the version history is the audit trail.

### 2.7 — Intelligence Feed
**What it is.** The full browse/stream of all Primary Intelligence posts — the expanded "Latest from Stout." Filterable by scope (Engagement / Company / Trial), title, date, with a "Publish intelligence" action for owner/editor.

**Say this.** *(brief — mention in passing)*
> "And every read Stout publishes lands in one filterable feed — the running record of the firm's thinking on this account."

### 2.8 — Materials / Files
**What it is.** The engagement's document library: every deliverable and source doc, categorized and **linked to the entities it's about** so it surfaces where the client is already looking.

**Why it matters.** Deliverables stop getting buried in inboxes and shared drives. A briefing about a trial shows up *on that trial*.

**Know this.**
- **TYPE categories:** Briefing (recurring client deliverable) · Conference report (write-up from a congress — AHA = American Heart Association, ObesityWeek) · Priority notice (urgent alert) · Ad hoc (one-off).
- **ENTITY filter:** Trial / Marker / Company / Asset / Engagement.
- **Two upload paths:** central "Register material" (link to multiple entities at once) and a contextual drop zone on every detail page that pre-fills the link to that entity.
- File-type badges: PowerPoint / PDF / Word / other.

**Say this.**
> "Every deliverable gets filed against what it's about — a briefing on a trial attaches to that trial, so it turns up right where the client is already reading, and again in one library that gathers everything. Nothing lives in an email."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"Can I fix a wrong link after upload?"* → Honest: editing links after upload is a near-term add today; for now it's delete + re-upload. *(Don't volunteer this — only if asked.)*

### 2.9 — Events / Activity
**What it is.** The change-monitoring feed — the full version of Home's "What changed." A detailed, searchable log of every change across the engagement.

**Why it matters.** This is the engine behind "always current without the busywork." It's what makes "live" true.

**Know this.**
- **SOURCE = Detected** means auto-found (mostly from ClinicalTrials.gov syncs); manual events are the alternative.
- **Change categories:** Timeline (date slips: "event date delayed N days, old → new") · Phase (PHASE2 → PHASE3) · Trial status (Recruiting → Active, not recruiting) · Catalyst lifecycle (marker added/removed) · Protocol design (enrollment target, arms, intervention).
- Powers the "Trial moves" signal on Home.

**Say this.**
> "Behind all of this is a daily sync against ClinicalTrials.gov. When a trial's date slips, a phase advances, a status changes — Clint catches it, logs it with the old and new value and the source, and it flows through to the home page and the timeline automatically. Nobody rewrites the picture by hand; it keeps itself current."

### 2.10 — Source Import (the live AI feature)
**What it is.** The AI on-ramp: paste a list of **NCT IDs**, a **URL** (e.g. a press release), or a **block of text**, hit fetch-and-resolve, and Clint's AI turns it into a structured landscape — companies, assets, trials, upcoming catalysts. **This is the one AI capability that is live today** (the other two are roadmap).

**Why it matters.** It collapses hours of blank-page assembly into minutes, and it's the proof point for "the data is built for AI."

**Know this.**
- Three input modes: NCT ID list · URL · free text.
- Every resolved entity traces back to its source (provenance).
- This is also the BD-pitch superpower (Slide 3): build a prospect's landscape before the pitch.

**Say this.**
> "Here's the part that's genuinely AI, and it's live today. I paste a few trial IDs — or a press release, or just a block of text — and Clint's AI builds the competitive landscape from it: the companies, their assets, the trials, the catalysts coming up. What used to be a morning of manual setup is ready to review in minutes, and every entry traces back to where it came from. For a pitch, this means you walk in with the prospect's whole landscape already built."

**You'll be asked.**
- *"How accurate is it?"* → It builds a draft an analyst reviews — it does the heavy lifting, the analyst confirms. Provenance on every entry makes review fast. Underpromise: it's an accelerator, not an oracle.
- *"What about the AI inside the platform / in my own assistant?"* → Roadmap. Be clear about the line.

And Stout runs all of it from one portal: stand up a new client, set their branding, manage who has access. No IT ticket. The client sees their own product; Stout's the one delivering it."

**What it is.** The reassurance layer for the client's security team (Slide 7).

**Know this (enough to be credible, not a CISO).**

- **Backups:** encrypted, daily, across two separate clouds, with restores rehearsed regularly.

### 2.14 — Data model surfaces (Companies / Assets / Trials / Marker Types / Taxonomies)
*Mostly for your own confidence — you likely won't demo these, but you should know them if asked "how does data get in / get configured."*
- **Companies / Assets / Trials** are the management surfaces. Company "Order" is a manual `display_order` that sets the row/wedge order across every view (the analyst's house ranking of competitors).
- **Trial vs. Asset phase (people conflate these):** a *trial* has a Phase that tops out at P4/Observational — a trial is never "approved." An *asset* has a Development Status (Preclinical → P1 → P2 → P3 → P4 → Approved → Launched), tracked **per indication** (a drug can be Launched for obesity but P3 for heart failure). Bullseye rings and heatmap columns use the *asset* status.
- **Marker Types:** 13 built-in types = shape + color + fill + inner mark. Color = category (fixed, never whitelabeled). Custom types can be added per space.
- **Taxonomies:** three controlled vocabularies — Indications (diseases), MOA, ROA — the source lists behind every dropdown, filter, and grouping.

## Part 3 — Objection handling

**1. "Won't clients see how the sausage is made, and stop needing us?"**
> "The deck was always a proxy for the value, not the value itself — what clients pay for is judgment. A live platform commoditizes the *assembly* work. It doesn't commoditize the read — unless assembly was all you were ever delivering. And it actually makes Stout *more* present: instead of going quiet for ten weeks between deliverables, your annotated read is in front of the client every week. The switching cost goes up, not down — a year of Stout's watchlists, annotations, and history living in their environment is hard to walk away from."

**2. "Why don't we just build this ourselves?"**
> "You could. Realistically that's two senior engineers, a data engineer, a pharma SME, and a PM — call it two-plus years and a few million to reach where this is today, plus running a product team in pharma data tooling, which isn't Stout's edge. Or you start on a live engagement next week and keep your people on client work. Build-versus-buy here isn't really about cost — it's about focus."

**3. "What does it cost?"** *(only give the number if pushed)*
> "$25K per engagement space, capped at $300K across the whole firm — so it scales with your book, and past about a dozen engagements new ones are effectively free. It's less than a single Evaluate seat, and it bundles into your engagement fee as cost of goods, not a SaaS line item the client ever sees."

**4. "Is it really live / is this just screenshots?"**

## Part 4 — Domain cheat-sheet

**Clinical trial phases (the spine):**
- **Preclinical** — lab/animal, pre-human.
- **Phase 1** — first in humans; safety/dosing; small.
- **Phase 2** — does it work? efficacy signal + safety; medium.
- **Phase 3** — large, pivotal, supports approval. **P3 readouts are the highest-stakes catalysts.**
- **Filed → Approved → Launched** — submitted to the regulator, approved, on the market.
- **LOE (Loss of Exclusivity)** — patent/exclusivity expiry; generics/biosimilars enter.

**The marker color key (memorize the mapping):**
- **Green = data readouts:** Topline (first headline result) · Interim (planned mid-trial look) · Full Data (complete dataset/publication).
- **Slate = trial milestones:** Primary Completion Date (PCD — primary-endpoint data collection finishes; precedes topline) · Trial Start · Trial End.
- **Orange = regulatory:** Filing · Submission · Acceptance (regulator agrees to review — starts the clock).
- **Blue = approval.** **Violet = launch.** (Approval ≠ launch — can be weeks/months apart.)
- **Amber = loss of exclusivity:** LOE Date · Generic Entry Date (the revenue cliff).
- **Fill = confidence:** filled = confirmed/actual · hollow = projected · struck-through = No Longer Expected.

**Vocabulary that will come up:**
- **Topline** — first high-level results of a trial's primary endpoint, before full publication. The single most-watched moment; stock and competitive read move on it.
- **Primary completion** — date the trial finishes collecting primary-endpoint data (precedes topline).
- **Confirmed vs. Projected** — a known/announced date vs. an estimate. Core data-quality signal, everywhere.
- **MoA (mechanism of action)** — how a drug works (GLP-1 receptor agonist, GIP/GLP-1 dual agonist, triple agonist).
- **RoA (route of administration)** — how it's given (SC = subcutaneous injection, PO = oral). Oral-vs-injectable is a major competitive axis.
- **Indication** — the disease a drug targets (obesity, T2D, heart failure, HCM, CKD, ATTR-CM).
- **NCT number** — a trial's ClinicalTrials.gov registry ID.
- **NDA / BLA / MAA** — the marketing applications filed with regulators (NDA = small-molecule US, BLA = biologic US, MAA = EU).
- **PDUFA date** — the FDA's target decision date once it accepts a filing.
- **Head-to-head** — a trial testing a drug directly against a competitor (e.g. MAPLE-HCM).
- **HCM** — hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (a heart-muscle disease).

**Commonly-confused pairs (have these straight):**
- **PCD vs. Trial End** — primary-endpoint data done vs. *everything* (incl. follow-up) done.
- **Interim / Topline / Full** — planned peek → first headline → complete dataset.
- **Filing/Submission vs. Acceptance** — sent vs. regulator agreed to review.
- **Approval vs. Launch** — allowed to sell vs. actually selling.
- **LOE vs. Generic Entry** — exclusivity expires vs. generics actually arrive.
- **Trial Phase vs. Asset Development Status** — a trial maxes at P4; an asset goes through to Approved/Launched, per indication.
