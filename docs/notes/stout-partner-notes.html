01 / Origin and IP

What was built, by whom, with what.

This needs to be airtight before the MD conversation, because the COI disclosure is only credible if the IP picture is clean.

Origin
  • The partner (Stout Strategy, Health Care Practice; ownership stake in Stout) flagged recurring pain points and suggested someone build a tool around them.
  • The founder built Clint independently. Code, platform, brand, and IP belong to the founder.
  • The partner contributes ideas, validation, and ongoing advisory. Not co-development.
IP ownership
  • Code, copyright, trademarks, and data model belong to the founder.
  • Ideas aren't protectable IP under US law. Implementation is. Nothing the partner contributed creates a Stout claim on the asset.
  • Required before any Stout-side conversation: founder/advisor agreement. Formalizes the partner's stake. Confirms in writing that no Stout time, systems, client data, or deliverables were used to build the platform.
  • This is cheap insurance. It's also what makes the COI disclosure on the next slide credible.

02 / The MD conversation, in seven steps

Exploratory, peer-to-peer, honest about state.

  1. Open with the strategic problem we've been talking about. "We've talked before about how we need to create more stickiness, and find something that differentiates us from the other strategy consulting firms, especially on the longer engagements where there's more money on the table. I think this is a real candidate for that."
  2. Frame the origin briefly. "I floated the idea a while back. [Founder] took it and built it. He owns the IP. Let me show you what it is."
  3. What it is, in one sentence. "It's a whitelabel product that gives customers live competitive data and intelligence the moment it's created. Data at their fingertips, with a growing interactive toolset on top."
  4. What's built today. Live multi-tenant platform. Whitelabel branding at agency, tenant, and engagement level, with custom domains. Structured event model with typed, dated, source-cited records. AI-ready data shape with exports available today and API/MCP integration on the roadmap.
  5. Where it fits at Stout. Internal: assembly compression. Client-facing: branded live environment, not a static deck. Strategic: ahead of the advisory market on AI-ready CI.
  6. The ask. 30-minute walkthrough with the founder. If it lands, scope a paid pilot on one live engagement.
  7. Soft "why now." "He's going to keep building this. Wanted Stout to have first look." Don't threaten. Just signal the optionality is real.
  8. If they ask about the long-term shape. Default Year 2+ is a regular customer relationship with full support, suggesting features, and shaping roadmap priority. If Stout wants exclusivity in advisory so peers can't have it, that's a separate conversation. Don't pitch the exclusivity; mention it if asked. "From Year 2 you'd be on regular pricing with full support, suggesting features and shaping what gets built. If you want exclusivity in advisory so other firms can't use this, that's a separate conversation we can have when it makes sense. Happy to discuss, not the entry point."

03 / What NOT to say in the first conversation

Three things to keep off the table.

  • No price. If asked, redirect: "Pricing is shaped from how Stout would actually use it. Let's get through the walkthrough first." The MD will benchmark to Evaluate ($20–50K/seat, $100K+/yr enterprise) the moment a number lands. Don't hand them that comparison.
  • No exclusivity offer. That's a structured discussion after the pilot, with the right people in the room. Don't promise it in the hallway.
  • No "we're talking to your competitors." Even if it's true. The whole point of the partner channel is that this is offered to Stout first.

06 / Exclusivity without giving up IP

Stout gets a moat. The founder keeps the asset.

  • License the right to use, not the IP itself. Standard SaaS framing. Clint retains all IP. Stout gets a non-transferable, non-sublicensable right to use within agreed scope.
  • Whitelabel isn't IP transfer. Stout's branding sits on the surface clients see. The platform, code, data model, and brand below the whitelabel stay with Clint.
  • Exclusivity is a contract term, not an IP transfer. Time-bound, category-bound, fully reversible. "Clint will not license to other firms in [defined advisory category] during the [2–3 year] term."
  • Reverse termination right. If Stout fails to renew or hit a usage minimum, exclusivity lapses immediately. Protects against Stout buying exclusivity, parking the asset, and killing its market.
  • Define the category narrowly. "Global advisory firms whose primary offering includes pharma transaction support, valuation, or CI consulting." Pharma and biotech direct sales are explicitly carved out.
  • No source code, no data ownership transfer. Even under exclusivity. Stout gets to use the platform, not to fork it.

08 / Competitor landscape (for reference)

What each platform does, and where buyers complain.

Reference for the partner. Useful if the MD compares Clint to Evaluate, Citeline, GlobalData, or others.

Focus areas
  • Evaluate. Sales forecasts, commercial data, epi data. Most commercial-leaning.
  • Citeline (formerly Pharma Intelligence). R&D, clinical trials, early research. PinkSheet "unparalleled" for regulatory milestones.
  • GlobalData. Broad coverage overlapping Evaluate + Citeline. Quality issues
  • Cortellis. Preclinical/clinical, patents, regulatory. Multiple ownership changes.
  • DRG. BD deal comps, NPV, market sizing.
  • TA Scan, Hanson Wade, BioWorld. Better data quality / niche modalities (ADC, bispecific) / news, respectively.
Buyer pain points
  • Clunky interfaces. Universal complaint across the big three.
  • Stale data. GlobalData specifically. "Miss at least 20% of updated information if you trust just one platform."
  • Multi-platform redundancy. Buyers pay for two or more and manually cross-check.
  • Aggressive sales tactics. GlobalData. "The most aggressive, obnoxious salesforce in our biz."
  • Opaque pricing. No published prices, every deal bespoke.
  • Vendor instability. Cortellis ownership has changed multiple times.

The wedge: Clint isn't pitched to replace Evaluate or Citeline at depth. It's the layer where Stout's curated, annotated view sits on top of those sources, with the strategic read attached.

09 / If the MD asks "why don't we just build this?"

Build-vs-buy: what it would cost Stout to do this themselves.

Reference only. Don't volunteer this; it's the answer if the MD pushes on whether Stout could build it in-house or outsource it.

Option A · Build in-house

$2–4M, 12–18 months

Team to reach Clint's current state: 2 senior full-stack engineers, 1 data engineer, 1 pharma domain SME, 1 PM/designer. Plus 6–12 months to hire the team before the clock starts.

Ongoing: $1–2M / yr (5 FTEs at loaded cost) plus pharma data licensing if integrating Evaluate / Citeline / GlobalData feeds.

Hidden cost: running a product engineering team isn't Stout's core competency. The Stout resources managing it would be doing that instead of client work.

Option B · Outsource to agency

$800K–1.5M build, then $400–800K / yr

Lower hourly rates ($60–150/hr offshore vs. $200+/hr in-house), but pharma domain expertise is rare offshore. 12–18 months to comparable functionality.

IP friction: contracting carefully so Stout actually owns what they pay for. Standard templates aren't enough for a strategic asset.

Sustained dependency: the agency becomes a permanent vendor relationship. No internal capability built. Switching costs grow over time.

Option C · Partner with Clint

Free pilot, then partnership pricing

Working product on Day 1. Free 90-day pilot. Discounted Year 1. Regular pricing Year 2+. Founder owns the asset and runs the team; brings the pharma domain expertise.

Stout's voice on direction: regular pricing from Year 2 with full product support, plus the ability to suggest features and influence roadmap priority, without owning the engineering burden.

Focus: Stout's edge is strategic advice, not running pharma data tooling. This keeps Stout resources on client work.
