## 1. Context and framing

### Origin

- Partner at Stout Strategy, Health Care Practice (ownership stake in the firm) flagged the recurring stickiness / differentiation problem and suggested someone build a tool around it.
- Founder built Clint independently. Code, platform, brand, and IP belong to the founder.
- Partner contributes ideation, domain validation, and ongoing advisory -- not co-development.

### IP ownership (clean)

- Code, copyright, trademarks, data model, and platform = founder.
- Partner's contributions = ideas and pain-point articulation. Under US IP law, ideas aren't protectable -- implementation is. Nothing the partner contributed creates a Stout claim on the asset.
- **Mitigation before any Stout-side conversation:** founder/advisor agreement that makes the structure explicit:
  - Founder is sole IP owner.
  - Partner is an advisor with [X]% equity (or revenue share, or option pool) vesting over [Y] years.
  - Partner contributed ideas and validation, not Stout-paid work product.
  - No Stout time, Stout systems, Stout client data, or Stout deliverables were used to build the platform. Confirmed in writing.

### On the partner's stake

- Partner has Stout equity (existing) and will have Clint upside (new). Partners at advisory firms regularly introduce tools and services they have economic relationships with -- it isn't unusual.
- **Don't lead with disclosure.** The partner is introducing a tool that helps Stout's clients. That's the framing.
- **If asked directly, be honest.** Brief and short: yes, there's a small stake; the introduction is happening because the partner thinks it's the right tool, not because of the stake.
- **Don't preemptively recuse.** Stout has its own internal COI processes. They'll apply those if they think it's necessary -- not for the partner to volunteer in advance.

## 2. Talking points for the partner -> MD conversation

This is the brief the partner uses, not the deck the MD reads. The MD-facing artifact is `stout-briefing.html`.

### Tone

- **Exploratory, not sales.** "Want to show you something I've been thinking about" beats "want to pitch you a product."
- **Insider-to-insider.** The MD will respond to a peer asking for 30 minutes, not to a vendor demo.
- **Honest about state.** Underpromise. The asset is real but early; the value is in where it can go with Stout's input.

### The flow (in order)

**1. Open with the strategic problem we've been talking about.**
- "We've talked before about how we need to create more stickiness, and find something that differentiates us from the other strategy consulting firms -- especially on the longer engagements where there's more money on the table. I think this is a real candidate for that."
- Anchors the conversation in a problem the MD has already named.

**2. Frame the origin briefly.**
- "I floated the idea a while back. [Founder] took it and built it. He owns the IP. Let me show you what it is."
- Don't volunteer the stake. If asked, answer honestly.

**3. What it is, in one sentence.**
- "It's a whitelabel product that gives customers live competitive data and intelligence the moment it's created -- at their fingertips, with a growing interactive toolset on top."

**4. What's built today.**
- Live multi-tenant platform with whitelabel branding at agency, tenant, and engagement level, plus custom domains.
- Structured event model: catalysts, regulatory actions, approvals, label changes, deal activity -- typed, dated, source-cited records.
- AI-ready data shape with exports available today and API/MCP integration on the roadmap.

**5. Where it fits at Stout.**
- *Internal:* less assembly, more analysis. Junior time on assembly compresses; senior time on interpretation grows.
- *Client-facing:* the deliverable is a Stout-branded site the client uses through the engagement, not a deck that goes stale in three weeks.
- *Strategic:* differentiated offering. No advisory firm delivers CI this way today.

**6. The ask.**
- A 30-minute walkthrough with the founder.
- If it lands, scope a free 90-day pilot on one live engagement.
- Not asking for: a procurement decision, a contract, an exclusivity discussion in this meeting.

**7. Soft "why now."**
- "He's going to keep building this. Wanted Stout to have first look." Don't threaten -- just signal the optionality is real.

**8. If they ask about the long-term shape.**
- The vision isn't "Stout as customer." It's Stout as co-creator on Clint -- exclusivity in the advisory category, equity participation, and a real seat shaping where the platform goes for the market.
- "The bigger picture is Stout coming in as a co-creator on this. Exclusivity in advisory, a seat in shaping the roadmap, the ability to build what the market needs. Not asking you to commit to that today -- just framing where this could go."

### What NOT to say in the first conversation

- **No price.** The MD will instinctively benchmark to Evaluate ($20--50K/seat, $100K+/yr enterprise). Pricing comes after they've seen the product.
- **No exclusivity offer.** That's a structured discussion after the pilot, with the right people in the room.
- **No "we're talking to your competitors."** Even if true. The whole point of the partner channel is that this is offered to Stout first.

## 3. Engagement structures

The grey area: partner needs Stout to engage so the asset doesn't go to a competitor; founder needs to retain IP and pharma-direct upside; Stout needs enough exclusivity and influence to feel they're getting something competitors can't have. The recommended structure (option 04 below) addresses all three.

### Options matrix

| # | Option | Structure | Cost to Stout | What founder keeps | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Annual license, non-exclusive** | Practice or firm license, no exclusivity | $75K--$500K/yr | Right to sell to anyone | Cleanest. Stout's competitors can also buy it. |
| 2 | **Category-exclusive license** | License + exclusivity within the *advisory/consulting* category for 2--3 years | License + $150K--$500K/yr exclusivity premium | Pharma/biotech direct sales remain open | Lighter-touch alternative to #4. Same exclusivity but no equity, no roadmap input. |
| 3 | **Vertical-exclusive license** | Stout exclusive across all advisory verticals (consulting, IB, PE) | Higher than #2 | Pharma direct only | Over-broad. PE/IB shops aren't competing with Stout on CI engagements. |
| 4 | **Co-creator partnership** *(recommended)* | Equity stake + category exclusivity in advisory + formal seat shaping the roadmap | Equity check + reduced license fee | Operational control, IP, ability to sell into pharma/biotech direct | Strongest alignment with Stout's stated need for stickiness and differentiation. Cash-for-equity, never IP-for-equity. |
| 5 | **Joint venture** | Co-owned entity sells into both markets | Setup + revenue split | 50% upside, partial control | Slow, expensive to structure, governance complexity. Skip. |

### Recommended sequence

1. **Free 90-day pilot.** No payment required. Live engagement on a real Stout client. Both sides see what works. Pilot ends with a delivered artifact the client can use.
2. **Discounted Year 1.** Convert into a Year 1 engagement at a partnership rate. Pricing reflects how Stout actually used Clint during the pilot.
3. **Co-creator partnership at Year 2+.** Regular pricing kicks in alongside the structural conversation: equity, exclusivity, roadmap seat. Option 04 above becomes the long-term shape.

### Exclusivity without losing IP -- the playbook

- **License the right to use, never the IP itself.** Standard SaaS framing. Clint retains all IP; Stout gets a non-transferable, non-sublicensable right to use within agreed scope.
- **Whitelabel != IP transfer.** Stout's branding sits on the surface clients see. Platform, code, data model, and brand below the whitelabel stay with Clint.
- **Exclusivity is a contract term, not an IP transfer.** "Clint will not license to other firms in [defined advisory category] during the [2--3 year] term." Time-bound, category-bound, fully reversible.
- **Reverse termination right.** If Stout fails to renew or fails to hit a usage minimum, exclusivity lapses immediately. Protects against the "buy and park" failure mode.
- **Carve-outs from day one.** Pharma/biotech direct sales explicitly permitted. Define the category narrowly: "global advisory firms whose primary service offering includes pharma transaction support, valuation, or competitive intelligence consulting."
- **No source code, no data ownership transfer.** Even under exclusivity. Stout gets to use the platform, not to fork it.
- **Founder agreement before any Stout-side conversation.** Documents the partner's contribution as advisory, not work-for-hire. Neutralizes any future "Stout incubated this" claim.

## 4. The product, in talking points

### What Clint is

- **Pharma-native data model.** Pipelines, assets, clinical trials, catalysts, and portfolios are stored as structured records, not free-text notes or PDFs.
- **Event-centric.** Every change -- readouts, regulatory actions, approvals, label changes, deal activity -- attaches to the right asset as a typed, dated, source-cited record.
- **Visual hierarchy.** Phase bars, color-coded markers, and a company-and-product hierarchy let a reader find the competitive read in seconds.
- **One data source, multiple consumers.** Interface, exports, briefing decks, client notices, and AI workflows all read from the same source.
- **AI-ready data shape.** Normalization and citations at ingest time mean the data is already in the form retrieval and generation pipelines need.

### What it means for Stout

- **Live data.** The competitive landscape on any asset or therapeutic area is current to the last event ingested. The strategic read sits on data that's current.
- **AI workflows.** Today: Stout analysts can export structured data from Clint into Claude, Copilot, or whatever AI tools the firm uses. On the roadmap: tenant API and MCP server. Later: Clint-native generation.
- **Whitelabel deliverable.** Each client engagement can run as a Stout-branded Clint site at a custom domain.
- **Engagement model.** Recurring CI work today is delivered quarterly as a deck. With Clint, the deliverable is a Stout-branded environment the client uses through the quarter, with Stout's analysis layered into it.
- **Position.** No advisory firm delivers CI this way today. Stout would be first.

## 5. Supporting context

### 5a. The "won't clients see how the pudding is made?" question

#### The risk

- The deck was never the value -- it was a proxy for it. What clients pay for is judgment.
- A live platform commoditizes assembly work. It does not commoditize the strategic read -- unless Stout was only ever delivering assembly work.
- If the strategic read is real, the live platform makes it more visible, not less.

#### How to make Stout's IP visible inside the platform

- **Curation as a deliverable.** Watchlist, competitor set, asset prioritization -- Stout's domain expertise applied.
- **Annotation as a deliverable.** Every meaningful event carries a Stout note: why it matters, what it shifts.
- **Strategy surface as a deliverable.** Scenario analysis, deal implications, recommendations -- branded as Stout's work.
- **Decks shrink, not disappear.** Quarterly briefings become "here is what we recommend you do."

#### Why visibility usually helps, not hurts

- Continuous activity is perceived as more value than one polished deck per quarter.
- Quarterly cadence has a known failure mode: clients forget you exist for ten weeks.
- Live, annotated environment keeps Stout present in the client's workflow every week.

#### Where the risk does bite -- and how to defuse it

- **Pricing posture.** Never expose the platform as a SaaS line item. Bundle inside the engagement.
- **Sales framing.** Position the platform as Stout's instrument, not the product.
- **Switching cost rises.** Watchlists, annotations, history, and scenario models in the Stout-branded environment = leaving costs institutional memory.

### 5b. Competitor landscape -- pricing and focus (from buyer chatter)

Source: r/biotech thread "Thoughts on pharma intel databases like Evaluate, GlobalData, etc."

#### Pricing data points

- **Evaluate** -- "five digits" annual for sub-500-employee company.
- **GlobalData** -- "six digits" at the same employer for a comparable product.
- **Per seat** -- "$20--$50K per seat is typical."
- **Enterprise** -- "hundreds of thousands... if not million-plus" at major pharma.
- **Pricing transparency** -- "I can't tell if I'm getting a great deal or getting ripped off."

#### Focus areas

| Platform | Primary focus | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Evaluate | Sales forecasts, commercial data, epi | Most commercial-leaning |
| Citeline | R&D, clinical trials, early research | PinkSheet "unparalleled" for regulatory milestones |
| GlobalData | Broad coverage | Quality issues -- "inaccurate or out of date" |
| Cortellis | Preclinical/clinical, patents, regulatory | Multiple ownership changes |
| DRG | BD deal comps, NPV, market sizing | Specifically for BD workflows |
| TA Scan | Competitive landscape, site ID | Better data quality than incumbents |
| Hanson Wade | Niche modality (ADC, bispecific) | Deep but narrow |
| BioWorld | News-leaning | Good for ongoing coverage |

#### Pain points

- **Clunky interfaces** -- universal.
- **Stale data** -- GlobalData specifically; "miss at least 20% of updated information" if you trust just one.
- **Forced multi-platform redundancy** -- buyers run two and manually cross-check.
- **Aggressive sales tactics** -- GlobalData specifically.
- **Opaque pricing** -- every deal bespoke.
- **Vendor instability** -- Cortellis ownership churn.

#### What this tells us for Clint's positioning

- **Coverage gap is the wedge.** Clint doesn't need to beat Evaluate at depth -- it needs to be the layer where Stout's curated, annotated view sits on top.
- **UX is a real differentiator.** Buyers themselves describe these as clunky.
- **Provenance is price-of-entry.** Source citations on every event are required for advisory firm output.
- **Whitelabel + AI-ready** is the combination none of them offer.

## 6. Build vs. buy -- if the MD asks why Stout shouldn't just build it

Reference for the partner. Don't volunteer this; it's the answer if the MD pushes on whether Stout could build internally or outsource.

### Option A -- Build in-house

- **Team to reach Clint's current state:** 2 senior full-stack engineers, 1 data engineer, 1 pharma domain SME, 1 PM/designer. Plus 6--12 months of hiring before the clock starts.
- **Build cost:** ~$2--4M, 12--18 months minimum.
- **Ongoing:** $1--2M/yr loaded for 5 FTEs, plus pharma data licensing if integrating Evaluate/Citeline/GlobalData feeds.
- **Hidden cost:** running a product engineering team isn't Stout's core competency. Senior people managing it are not doing client work.

### Option B -- Outsource to an agency

- Lower hourly rates ($60--150/hr offshore vs. $200+/hr in-house), but pharma domain expertise is rare offshore.
- **Build cost:** $800K--1.5M for comparable functionality, 12--18 months.
- **Ongoing:** $400--800K/yr maintenance contracts.
- **IP friction:** standard templates aren't enough for a strategic asset; contracting needs care.
- **Sustained dependency:** agency becomes a permanent vendor; no internal capability built.

### Option C -- Partner with Clint

- Working product on Day 1. Free 90-day pilot. Discounted Year 1. Regular pricing Year 2+.
- Founder owns the asset and runs the team; pharma domain expertise comes with it.
- Co-creator partnership at Year 2 gives Stout exclusivity in advisory and a seat shaping the roadmap, without owning the engineering burden.
- Stout's senior time stays on client work.

### Bottom line

Build-vs-buy isn't just cost. It's focus. Stout's edge is strategic advice, not running a product engineering team in pharma data tooling.
